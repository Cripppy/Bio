Chromosome Structure and Karyotyping

Chromosomes are made of chromatin and chromatin consists of DNA and protein. The chromosome itself is really an intense packaging.It starts with DNA being round around proteins called histones. This forms nucleosomes which are often considered to look like beans. More and more condense packaging occur until it results to a chromosome-level packaging. 

In your cells, we have a lot of DNA and it has to fit inside the nucleus proving the importance of condense packaging. Human chromosomes can be a single component or they can be replicated. The chromosomes are duplicated during Interphase, the newly-made chromosomes bound in the region called the centromere. But this region doesn't technically need to be in the center of the chromosome. Because of this joining, we still count it as one chromosome unit. To fully understand, we can say that there are two sister chromatids. 

In humans, we have 46 chromosomes. In Interphase, they are duplicated, still we have 46 chromosomes but 92 chromatids. During the stage of mitosis, those 92 chromatids get pulled apart.

In a karyotype, the chromosomes are stained and visible. The chromosomes from a karyotype are typically from a cell that is in mitosis stage, typically metaphase as chromosomes are condensed and thick. For optimal viewing, a typical karyotype is taken at or right before metaphase. When arranging a karyotype, the chromosomes are arranged in homologous pairs. Homologous chromosomes are about the same size and contain the same types of genes. In each homologous pair, you receive one from one parent and one chromosome from the other parent.

The 22 pairs(44 chromosomes) are called autosomes. This means they are not related to the biological sex. They may have genes related to the eye color, height, or texture. The last two chromosomes are called sex chromosomes because they determine your biological sex. Females have XX chromosomes. Males have XY chromosomes. 

You may ask that why do we care about karyotypes or learning about chromosomes? The reason is it helps us understand genetic disorders. Karyotypes can reveal missing or extra chromosomes at specific locations.
